# ENE 13.05i Major Task 2
## Content and argument draft

**Working title:** *For You, For Profit*  
**Format:** Research-based interactive website  
**Main platform focus:** TikTok and short-form recommendation feeds  
**Estimated experience:** 8 to 10 minutes  
**Status:** High-level content draft. The six game videos and final copy can still change.

---

## 1. Project context

This project examines how recommendation feeds turn ordinary attention into a resource. It focuses on TikTok because its For You Page is shaped heavily by behavioral signals such as follows, likes, viewing patterns, and explicit feedback. The website uses consumer content as its main example because the movement from interest to recommendation to purchase is easy to see and interact with.

The project does not argue that wanting things is wrong or that every recommendation is manipulation. A person may genuinely enjoy a product review, follow a creator, or look for something they need. The issue begins when the platform repeatedly redirects that interest toward engagement and consumption, while the process still feels personal and self-directed.

The interactive game presents a simplified version of this process. The research debrief then explains the technical, psychological, and social systems behind it.

---

## 2. Main thesis

Being human is not the problem. People have interests, desires, habits, and moments of impulse. Recommendation systems learn from these ordinary behaviors and use them to decide what comes next. On TikTok, a real interest can gradually become a feed filled with reviews, affiliate links, livestreams, vouchers, and advertisements.

This creates a grand narrative in which consumption appears to be the natural next step after having an interest. Users can resist this story through micro narratives: skipping content, using platform controls, choosing creators more carefully, questioning persuasive messages, waiting before buying, and seeking information outside the feed. These actions matter, but they take place inside a system whose ranking rules, commercial incentives, and moderation policies are controlled by the platform.

The project therefore makes two connected claims:

1. Users have real agency and can become more deliberate about what they watch, believe, share, and buy.
2. A fair digital system should not place the entire burden of resisting algorithmic influence on individual users.

---

## 3. Research question

**How can users exercise meaningful agency when recommendation systems use their attention to form commercial and social narratives around them?**

A secondary question guides the final sections:

**What responsibilities should platforms carry when users cannot fully control how content is ranked, repeated, disclosed, or removed?**

---

## 4. Target audience and intended impact

The immediate audience is the course professor, but the website should read as an advocacy and information product for everyday social media users, especially students and young adults familiar with short-form video feeds.

The goal is not to make the audience fear technology or delete TikTok. The goal is to make the hidden process easier to notice. After completing the experience, the user should understand:

- how ordinary actions become recommendation signals;
- how repetition can turn separate posts into a larger narrative;
- what platform controls are available;
- why awareness and judgment still matter after using those controls;
- why individual effort cannot solve a system-level problem by itself.

# Website content draft

## 5. Quick project introduction

**Target reading time:** about 10 seconds.

> Recommendation feeds do more than respond to what we like. They learn from our attention, arrange what comes next, and can slowly turn an interest into a story about what we should want. This website lets you experience one version of that process, then opens it up for examination.

This section gives the professor enough context before the more cinematic opening begins.

---

## 6. Opening sequence: how the feed changed

The website begins with a phone feed and a short sequence of past actions:

> You liked a video because it was funny.  
> You finished a review because you were curious.  
> You followed a creator whose style you liked.  
> You shared a recommendation with a friend.  
> You opened one product link.

The screen becomes increasingly crowded with reviews, affiliate posts, shop links, vouchers, livestreams, and advertisements.

> Then your feed stopped feeling like yours.

This sequence should not blame the user. The actions are normal. Research supports the idea that several kinds of interaction can affect TikTok recommendations. Boeker and Urman (2022) found that follows, likes, view rate, language, and location influenced the recommendations received by controlled accounts. Baumann et al. (2025) also found strong reinforcement of trained interests, often within the first 200 videos watched, together with a gradual decline in content diversity.

The website compresses this longer process into a short sequence. It should state clearly that the game is a research-informed simulation, not a recreation of TikTok's proprietary algorithm.

---

## 7. Grand and micro narratives

The project uses only two course terms.

### Grand narrative

A grand narrative is the larger story that makes a system or way of living feel normal. It is rarely contained in one post. It forms when similar messages keep appearing, support one another, and begin to feel like common sense.

The game focuses on this consumer grand narrative:

> Having an interest should lead to wanting more, comparing more, and eventually buying more.

A funny video, an honest review, an affiliate recommendation, and a brand advertisement may look different. When the feed arranges them into a repeated sequence, they can still help tell the same larger story.

### Micro narrative

A micro narrative is a smaller story created by individuals or communities that resists, questions, or rewrites the larger one.

In the game, the user's choices form a micro narrative:

> I can keep the interests that are mine without letting every interest become a purchase.

The micro narrative includes actions inside the platform, but it also includes how the user interprets the content and what they decide to do afterward.

---

## 8. Game setup

The user enters a guided, sales-heavy short-video feed. The task is simple:

> Take back the feed.

The game should contain around six fixed videos with a combined runtime of about two minutes. The exact videos remain open for production, but the set should include a clear range:

- genuine interest-based content;
- an obvious brand advertisement;
- a creator promotion with a visible disclosure;
- a subtler affiliate or commission-based recommendation;
- an unsponsored product review that still encourages consumption;
- repeated shop, voucher, haul, or livestream content.

The game is guided rather than fully open-ended. Each video reaches a decision point, and the user must choose an action before continuing. This guarantees that the professor sees every core idea within three to four minutes.

Possible actions include:

- like;
- follow or unfollow;
- continue;
- open the product;
- open more options;
- choose "Not Interested."

The choices do not need to produce a complex branching feed. They affect the simulation's meter and the final results. The point is to train recognition and deliberate action, not to reverse-engineer TikTok.

---

## 9. Feed drift meter

A visible meter tracks the direction of the simulated feed:

**Interest-led** ←→ **Sales-led**

The meter moves toward sales-led when the user gives stronger engagement to commercial content. It moves toward interest-led when the user rejects, skips, unfollows, or filters repeated selling.

This is a metaphor, not a scientific score. The result does not claim to know what TikTok would actually recommend. Its purpose is to show that a series of small interactions can accumulate into a larger direction.

The academic terms should not be forced into the meter itself. The debrief later explains that the sales-led drift represents the consumer grand narrative, while the user's deliberate choices help build a micro narrative against it.

---

## 10. Research feedback inside the game

Research should appear during the game without turning every interaction into a lecture.

After selected actions, the website can show one short line:

> Watching to the end can act as an implicit signal.

> "Not Interested" is an explicit signal, but the control is less visible than ordinary engagement actions.

> A clear label makes commercial intent easier to recognize.

Kaplan et al. (2026) found that TikTok's feed responded to both explicit and implicit signals. Their controlled accounts had difficulty removing unwanted topics over time, even after using the strongest explicit option, "Not Interested." The researchers also noted that this option is buried in the interface.

For commercial disclosure, Mathur et al. (2018) found that only about one in ten affiliate posts in their large YouTube and Pinterest sample contained a disclosure, and short or unexplained labels were often misunderstood. Boerman et al. (2017) found that sponsorship disclosures can activate persuasion knowledge, helping viewers recognize that a post is advertising.

Inline citations should appear in a small, readable format, such as **(Kaplan et al., 2026)**. A click or hover can open a short source note.

---

## 11. Feed-level controls

After the six videos, the game pauses.

> Individual posts are only one layer. You can also change the feed itself.

The user is shown a simplified control panel based on real platform tools:

- mark posts as "Not Interested";
- unfollow repetitive creators;
- manage topic frequency;
- filter keywords;
- refresh the For You feed;
- use a following-based feed more intentionally.

The goal is to show that agency is not limited to liking or ignoring individual posts. Users can make broader choices about what enters their feed.

The section must also state the limitation: these tools influence recommendations but do not give the user full control over the ranking system. Kaplan et al. (2026) found that unwanted topics often returned after controlled accounts stopped repeatedly signaling disinterest. Vera and Ghosh (2025) similarly documented user frustration and uneven success when trying to remove unwanted TikTok recommendations.

---

## 12. Results overview

The results page should summarize the user's playthrough without declaring that they "won" or "lost."

Possible results:

- final feed drift;
- commercial posts engaged with;
- advertisements correctly recognized;
- explicit feedback actions used;
- feed controls selected;
- implicit signals produced during the game.

The framing should remain neutral:

> You influenced the feed, but the feed also shaped the choices you were responding to.

The user should be able to reopen every video.

Each result card should include:

# Research debrief

## 14. What the system saw

The debrief begins by separating deliberate feedback from behavior that can still be interpreted as a signal.

### What the user deliberately communicated

- liked a post;
- followed or unfollowed a creator;
- selected "Not Interested";
- opened a product;
- changed a feed setting.

### What the system could also infer from behavior

- watch duration;
- completion or replay;
- quick skipping;
- repeated attention to a topic;
- which videos caused the user to pause;
- which types of content were ignored.

Boeker and Urman (2022) found that follow behavior, likes, and video view rate affected recommendations in their TikTok audit. Kaplan et al. (2026) directly compared implicit and explicit signals and found that both changed personalized content. The practical issue is that users can send implicit signals without intending to make a clear statement about what they want.

The debrief should avoid saying that the system "knows" the person in a human sense. It builds predictions from observed behavior. Those predictions may be useful, inaccurate, narrow, or persistent.

---

## 15. How the grand narrative formed

The game videos return as one visual sequence:

**interest → review → lifestyle → recommendation → urgency → purchase**

No single post has to command the user to buy. The narrative forms through arrangement and repetition.

The first post may reflect a genuine interest. The next shows a product connected to that interest. Another creator frames the product as useful, stylish, or socially normal. A voucher reduces the feeling of risk. A countdown creates urgency. The feed turns separate pieces of content into a path.

Baumann et al. (2025) found rapid reinforcement of interest-aligned content and a negative relationship between amplification and exploration of unseen hashtags. The study does not prove that every personalized feed becomes commercial, but it supports the broader point that repeated engagement can narrow what the user encounters.

The game uses consumerism because the direction is visible:

> You wanted one thing. The feed planned what came next.

The project should still preserve the more careful thesis:

> The system does not need to invent desire. It can capture a real interest and repeatedly redirect it toward consumption.

---

## 16. The micro narrative inside the platform

The user's platform-level actions form the first part of the micro narrative.

- refusing an unwanted recommendation;
- unfollowing a creator whose content has become repetitive;
- filtering a term;
- changing topic settings;
- refreshing the feed;
- choosing to use a following-based feed.

These actions matter because they interrupt the idea that the feed is fixed or fully outside the user's influence. The user can respond and make choices.

Still, this agency is uneven. Passive participation is easy. A person can pause, watch, replay, or click without opening a menu or making a deliberate decision. Resistance often requires several steps and repeated effort. Kaplan et al. (2026) found that the strongest explicit rejection signal was less accessible than ordinary engagement actions, and disfavored content could return when rejection stopped.

The project should not romanticize the micro narrative as one heroic click. It is usually a pattern of smaller decisions.

---

## 17. Agency beyond platform controls

Platform controls change what may appear next. They cannot decide what the user believes, shares, or buys. The next layer of agency is awareness.

### Persuasion knowledge

Friestad and Wright's (1994) Persuasion Knowledge Model explains that people develop knowledge about persuasion and use it to interpret and respond to attempts to influence them. In the context of the feed, this means asking:

- Is this post trying to sell something?
- Does the creator earn money if I buy?
- Is the recommendation based on evidence, or mainly trust in the creator?
- Did I want this before I saw it repeatedly?
- Is urgency being used to shorten the time I have to think?

Boerman et al. (2017) found that sponsorship disclosure can help activate persuasion knowledge. However, advertising literacy is not a perfect shield. Rozendaal et al. (2011) argued that recognizing advertising does not automatically prevent emotional or behavioral effects.

### Deliberate friction

The user can create a pause between exposure and action:

- wait before buying;
- leave the app before checking out;
- compare reviews outside the platform;
- check the creator's relationship with the brand;
- treat countdowns and vouchers as persuasion rather than proof of value;
- ask whether the product solves a real need.

Lyngs et al. (2019) reviewed 367 digital self-control tools and found many designs that add blocking, reminders, limits, or friction. Their work supports the idea that self-control is easier when the environment helps create a pause. The user should not be expected to rely on willpower alone.

A useful line for the website is:

> Feed controls help decide what appears. Critical awareness helps decide what deserves attention, belief, sharing, or money.

---

## 18. Beyond selling

The game focuses on buying, but the same narrative process can affect politics, public issues, identity, and social beliefs.

This section should stay brief. It is an extension of the argument, not a second case study.

### Selective visibility

Recommendation systems rank some posts above others. A feed therefore shows a selected version of what is happening, not a neutral sample of the world.

Bakshy et al. (2015) found that both Facebook's ranking system and users' own choices reduced exposure to politically cross-cutting news. The study is useful because it avoids an easy answer. The information environment is shaped by both platform curation and user behavior.

### Political recommendation

In a TikTok audit conducted around the 2024 United States presidential election, Ibrahim et al. (2025) found asymmetries in partisan recommendations across hundreds of controlled accounts. Republican-seeded accounts received more aligned recommendations than Democratic-seeded accounts, while Democratic-seeded accounts received more opposite-party content. The authors did not establish intentional favoritism or prove a change in voting behavior. Their findings still show that political recommendation outcomes can be uneven and deserve independent scrutiny.

### Emotional amplification

Piccardi et al. (2024) ran a preregistered field experiment on X in which participants received more or less content expressing partisan animosity and antidemocratic attitudes. Increased exposure worsened feelings toward the opposing party and increased negative emotions. Reduced exposure improved out-party feelings.

This matters because engagement systems can reward content that holds attention through anger, fear, or outrage. A post does not have to be an advertisement to benefit from the same attention-based logic.

### Moderation and disappearance

Narratives are shaped by what is repeated, but also by what is removed or made harder to see. Magdy et al. (2025) examined deleted Arabic posts related to the Palestine-Israel conflict and found a gap between Arab annotators' understanding of Facebook's standards and the platform's enforcement. The study raises a larger question: whose language, context, and interpretation shape moderation rules?

This section should avoid claiming that one platform intentionally hides an entire side of a conflict unless the evidence supports that claim. The safer and more important point is that ranking and moderation systems can affect which accounts, languages, and perspectives remain visible.

The transition back to agency can be:

> The feed can sell a product, but it can also normalize a worldview. That is why awareness cannot stop at recognizing advertisements.

---

## 19. Information agency

The micro narrative also includes how users handle information.

Possible actions:

- do not treat frequent content as proof that most people agree;
- check who created the post and what evidence it provides;
- verify political claims outside the platform;
- intentionally look for sources and perspectives outside the personalized feed;
- pause before sharing content designed to provoke anger or fear;
- separate visibility from truth.

Fazio et al. (2015) found that repeated statements can feel more truthful even when people have knowledge that contradicts them. Repetition increases familiarity, and familiarity can be mistaken for accuracy. This gives the grand narrative a psychological layer: what appears again and again may begin to feel normal or true simply because it is familiar.

The website should avoid suggesting that users can become immune by being "smart enough." Awareness helps, but attention is limited, emotional content works quickly, and no person can independently investigate every post they see.

---

## 20. Limits of individual agency

The project should be clear that personal responsibility matters without turning the problem into a lecture about self-discipline.

Users can:

- shape some recommendations;
- choose creators more carefully;
- create friction before purchases;
- verify information;
- refuse to share emotionally charged claims;
- leave the feed when it begins to narrow their attention.

Users cannot fully control:

- the ranking model;
- which signals receive the most weight;
- what commercial content is eligible for recommendation;
- whether disclosure rules are enforced;
- how moderation systems interpret language and context;
- how long an explicit preference remains effective;
- the business incentives behind engagement.

This produces the project's central tension:

> Users are neither powerless nor fully in control.

---

## 21. Systemic responsibility

The final analytical movement shifts from what users can do to what platforms should do.

A fairer system would include:

- recommendation controls that are visible and easy to use;
- clear explanations for why a post appears;
- settings that produce meaningful and lasting changes;
- obvious disclosure of sponsorships, gifts, and affiliate commissions;
- stronger separation between entertainment and shopping;
- transparent moderation and appeals;
- independent audits of political recommendation systems;
- design that does not depend on constant user vigilance.

Mathur et al. (2018) found widespread under-disclosure in affiliate content and poor understanding of short disclosure labels. Kaplan et al. (2026) found that even the most effective explicit feed control was buried and required repeated use. Together, these studies show why agency is partly a design issue. Platforms decide whether control is easy, visible, and durable.

The justice angle is therefore not abstract. It concerns meaningful consent, fair visibility, understandable commercial relationships, and the ability to contest automated decisions.

---

## 22. Final message

A possible ending:

> Your attention begins with you. The system decides what to do with it next.  
>  
> You can shape your feed. You can question the story it keeps repeating. You can pause before attention becomes belief, sharing, or spending.  
>  
> But meaningful agency should not require every user to constantly defend themselves from a system built to learn faster than they can react.

A shorter final line can appear alone:

> Keep your interests. Question who is arranging what comes next.
