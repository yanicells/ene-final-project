# For You, For Profit
## Direct website flow

**Target runtime:** 8 to 10 minutes  
**Game:** about 4 minutes  
**Research debrief:** about 4 to 5 minutes

---

## 1. Quick introduction

Give the user context before the animation.

> Recommendation feeds do more than respond to what we like. They learn from our attention, arrange what comes next, and can slowly turn an interest into a story about what we should want.

Button: **Begin**

---

## 2. Opening: how the feed changed

Show a phone while these lines appear:

> You liked a funny video.  
> You finished a product review.  
> You followed a creator.  
> You shared a recommendation.  
> You opened one product link.

The feed gradually fills with reviews, affiliate links, shop posts, vouchers, and ads.

> Then your feed stopped feeling like yours.

The user did nothing unusual. Follows, likes, view rate, and watch behavior can affect TikTok recommendations (Boeker & Urman, 2022). Interest-based content can also be reinforced quickly while feed diversity decreases (Baumann et al., 2025).

Add a small note:

> This is a simplified, research-informed simulation. It does not recreate TikTok's actual algorithm.

---

## 3. Grand and micro narratives

### Grand narrative

A larger story repeated until it starts to feel normal.

For the game:

> Having an interest should lead to wanting more, comparing more, and eventually buying more.

No single post tells this story. It forms when reviews, lifestyle content, shop links, and urgency keep appearing around the same interest.

### Micro narrative

A smaller story built through choices that question or resist the larger one.

For the user:

> I can keep my interests without letting every interest become a purchase.

Button: **Take back the feed**

---

## 4. Guided game

Use around six fixed videos with a combined runtime of about two minutes:

- normal interest-based content;
- an obvious ad;
- a creator promotion with clear disclosure;
- a subtler affiliate recommendation;
- an unsponsored product review;
- repeated shop, voucher, haul, or livestream content.

After each video, the user must choose an action:

- Like
- Follow or unfollow
- Continue
- Open product
- More options
- Not Interested

Everyone sees the same videos. The choices only affect the meter, feedback, and final results. The goal is not to reproduce a real recommender system. It is to make agency easy to experience.

---

## 5. Feed drift meter

Keep one visible meter:

**Interest-led** ←→ **Sales-led**

It moves toward sales-led when the user strongly engages with commercial content. It moves back when the user rejects, skips, unfollows, or filters repeated selling.

This is a metaphor, not a real TikTok score. The user should never be told that they failed.

---

## 6. Research feedback during the game

Show only short lines after selected actions:

> Watching to the end can act as an implicit signal.

> "Not Interested" is an explicit signal, but it is less visible than ordinary engagement actions.

> A clear sponsorship label helps viewers recognize commercial intent.

Kaplan et al. (2026) found that TikTok reacted to both implicit and explicit feedback. They also found that "Not Interested" was buried in the interface and often needed repeated use. Research on affiliate content also shows that commercial relationships are often poorly disclosed or misunderstood (Mathur et al., 2018).

---

## 7. Feed-level controls

After the six videos, show broader controls:

- Not Interested
- Unfollow repetitive creators
- Manage Topics
- Filter Keywords
- Refresh the For You feed
- Use Following more intentionally

Let the user select the controls they would use.

Then state the limit:

> These tools can influence the feed, but they do not give users full control over ranking.

Kaplan et al. (2026) found that unwanted topics could return after users stopped repeatedly signaling disinterest.

---

## 8. Results and video review

Show:

- final feed drift;
- ads recognized;
- commercial posts engaged with;
- explicit feedback used;
- implicit signals produced;
- feed controls selected.

Main line:

> You influenced the feed, but the feed also shaped the choices you were responding to.

The user can reopen every video and see:

- what they did;
- what signal it produced in the simulation;
- how it affected the meter;
- one short research note;
- an APA 7 citation.

---

# Research debrief

## 9. What the system saw

Separate deliberate actions from behavior that still carries information.

**What you deliberately told it**

- Like
- Follow
- Not Interested
- Product click
- Feed setting

**What behavior also revealed**

- Watch duration
- Completion
- Replay
- Quick skips
- Repeated attention
- Pauses

A recommendation system does not need to understand a person. It only needs enough signals to predict what may hold attention next. Boeker and Urman (2022) found that follows, likes, and video view rate changed recommendations in controlled TikTok accounts.

---

## 10. How the grand narrative formed

Bring back the videos as one sequence:

**Interest → Review → Lifestyle → Recommendation → Urgency → Purchase**

> No single video told the whole story. The narrative formed through what kept returning.

The first post may reflect a real interest. The next introduces a product. Another creator makes it feel normal. A voucher lowers hesitation. A countdown creates urgency.

Baumann et al. (2025) found strong reinforcement of interest-aligned content and less exploration as amplification increased.

Main argument:

> The system does not need to invent desire. It can capture a real interest and repeatedly redirect it toward consumption.

---

## 11. The micro narrative

The user resists through a pattern of smaller choices:

- rejecting unwanted content;
- unfollowing repetitive creators;
- changing topic settings;
- filtering keywords;
- checking outside the feed;
- waiting before buying.

The micro narrative is not one heroic click. It is repeated, deliberate judgment.

---

## 12. Agency beyond controls

Controls affect what may appear next. They cannot decide what the user believes, shares, or buys.

Friestad and Wright's (1994) Persuasion Knowledge Model explains that people respond better to influence when they recognize that persuasion is happening.

Use practical questions:

- Did I want this before I saw it repeatedly?
- Does the creator earn money if I buy?
- Is urgency shortening my decision?
- Have I checked other sources?
- Am I reacting because the post made me excited, angry, or afraid?

Possible actions:

- wait before buying;
- leave the app before checking out;
- compare reviews elsewhere;
- check sponsorships or affiliate links;
- pause before sharing emotional content.

Awareness helps, but it is not perfect protection. Recognizing advertising does not automatically remove its effects (Rozendaal et al., 2011).

Main line:

> Feed controls help decide what appears. Critical awareness helps decide what deserves attention, belief, sharing, or money.

---

## 13. Beyond selling

The game focuses on consumerism, but feeds can also shape politics, conflict, identity, and social beliefs.

> The feed can sell a product. It can also normalize a worldview.

Keep this brief:

- **Selective visibility:** Ranking decides what becomes easy to see. Both Facebook's algorithm and users' own choices reduced exposure to politically different news (Bakshy et al., 2015).
- **Political recommendation:** A TikTok audit found uneven partisan recommendation patterns during the 2024 U.S. election. It did not prove intentional favoritism, but it shows why audits matter (Ibrahim et al., 2025).
- **Emotional amplification:** Changing exposure to partisan hostility on X affected users' emotions and attitudes toward the opposing party (Piccardi et al., 2024).
- **Moderation:** What is removed also shapes the story. Moderation can affect which languages, contexts, and perspectives remain visible.

This is an extension, not a second case study.

---

## 14. Why awareness matters

Repeated claims can feel more believable because they become familiar. Fazio et al. (2015) found that repetition increased perceived truth even when participants already knew the correct answer.

Use four reminders:

- Popular does not mean true.
- Repeated does not mean important.
- Visible does not mean representative.
- Personalized does not mean neutral.

---

## 15. Limits of individual agency

**Users can control**

- some interactions;
- some settings;
- what they buy;
- what they share;
- where they verify information.

**Users cannot fully control**

- ranking rules;
- signal weights;
- platform incentives;
- disclosure enforcement;
- moderation systems;
- how long preferences remain effective.

> Users are neither powerless nor fully in control.

---

## 16. Systemic responsibility

A fairer platform would provide:

- visible recommendation controls;
- clear "Why this post?" explanations;
- lasting topic preferences;
- obvious sponsorship and affiliate disclosures;
- stronger separation between entertainment and shopping;
- transparent moderation and appeals;
- independent audits of political recommendation systems.

Individual awareness matters, but platform design decides whether control is easy, visible, and durable.

---

## 17. Final message

> Your attention begins with you. The system decides what to do with it next.  
>  
> Keep your interests. Question who is arranging what comes next.

Buttons:

- **View sources**
- **Replay experience**
